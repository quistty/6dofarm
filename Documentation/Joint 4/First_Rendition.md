# Joint 4: First CAD Rendition Review

## 1. Design Overview

Joint 4 was designed as the rotational connection between the Joint 3 structure and the combined Joint 5 and Joint 6 wrist module.

The design uses a belt-driven pulley system to rotate the complete Joint 5 and Joint 6 assembly around the Joint 4 axis.

The main components of the first rendition were:

- Joint 4 housing
- Joint 4 motor mount
- NEMA 17 stepper motor
- Motor drive pulley
- Joint 4 driven pulley
- Timing belt
- Joint 5 and Joint 6 housing holder
- Joint 4 support rods
- Joint 4 support bearings
- Joint 4 end support
- Joint 3 mounting structure

The overall mechanism was intentionally simple. The motor remained mounted to the Joint 4 structure while the belt transferred rotational motion to the Joint 5 and Joint 6 wrist assembly.

---

## 2. Joint 5 and Joint 6 Housing Support

The combined Joint 5 and Joint 6 housing was supported by bearings located inside the Joint 4 housing holder.

The purpose of these bearings was to allow the complete Joint 5 and Joint 6 wrist module to rotate around the Joint 4 axis while preventing direct contact between the rotating housing and the stationary Joint 4 structure.

The bearing arrangement was intended to be the only rotational contact between the two structures.

This means that:

- The Joint 5 and Joint 6 housing rotates through the bearings.
- The Joint 4 housing remains stationary relative to the motor.
- The rotating housing should not rub directly against the stationary support.
- Structural loads should be transferred through the bearings and their mounts.
- Other housing surfaces should maintain sufficient clearance during rotation.

The Joint 5 and Joint 6 housing holder was connected to the Joint 4 structure through rods positioned around the rotating assembly.

These rods allowed the bearing supports on both sides of the Joint 4 assembly to remain connected while leaving the centre of the mechanism available for the rotating wrist housing.

---

## 3. Support Rod Arrangement

The Joint 4 housing holder used four large support rods.

These rods extended approximately 100 mm between the front and rear sections of the Joint 4 support structure.

The rod length was intended to remain variable so that different rod lengths could be installed if the spacing between the Joint 4 supports needed to change.

The rods served several purposes:

- Maintain the spacing between the two support sections
- Keep the bearing supports aligned
- Connect the front and rear portions of the Joint 4 structure
- Resist separation between the supports
- Create an open frame around the rotating wrist assembly
- Allow the overall Joint 4 length to be adjusted

The rods were not intended to contact the rotating Joint 5 and Joint 6 housing.

Their purpose was only to connect and align the stationary support structures surrounding the rotating joint.

---

## 4. Joint 4 End Support

The end of the Joint 4 housing holder was supported using ball bearings.

These bearings supported the rotating Joint 5 and Joint 6 assembly while allowing the wrist module to rotate around the Joint 4 axis.

The bearing races must connect to the correct components:

- One race must connect to the rotating wrist housing.
- The opposite race must connect to the stationary Joint 4 support.
- The bearing must be retained so that it cannot move axially out of its seat.
- The surrounding structure must not clamp both races together and prevent rotation.

The first rendition established the general bearing-supported rotation concept, but the exact method of retaining the rotating housing was not fully developed.

---

## 5. Joint 4 Housing and Motor Mount

The Joint 4 motor housing was designed using a simple circular body with an extrusion for the motor mount.

The circular portion surrounded the Joint 4 rotational axis, while the motor-mount extrusion provided a flat surface for attaching the NEMA 17 stepper motor.

The motor mount included four mounting holes matching the standard mounting pattern of the NEMA 17 motor.

The motor was positioned so that its shaft remained parallel to the Joint 4 axis.

This allowed the motor pulley and Joint 4 driven pulley to be connected using a timing belt.

The simple shape of the housing was beneficial because it avoided unnecessary features and reduced the number of separate components required for the mechanism.

---

## 6. Belt and Pulley Drive

Joint 4 was driven using a belt connected between the NEMA 17 motor and the rotating Joint 5 and Joint 6 housing.

A drive pulley was mounted to the NEMA 17 motor shaft.

The pulley bore was sized to fit the motor shaft, while the outer pulley diameter was larger than the shaft so that the pulley teeth could properly engage the timing belt.

The timing belt connected the motor drive pulley to a larger driven pulley attached to the rotating Joint 5 and Joint 6 housing.

When the motor rotated:

1. The motor shaft rotated the drive pulley.
2. The drive pulley moved the timing belt.
3. The timing belt rotated the driven pulley.
4. The driven pulley rotated the complete Joint 5 and Joint 6 wrist assembly.
5. This rotation produced the movement of Joint 4.

The driven pulley must be rigidly connected to the rotating wrist housing so that the pulley cannot rotate independently from the housing.

The pulley ratio can also provide an additional reduction between the NEMA 17 motor and Joint 4.

## 7. Elements That Worked

The first rendition of Joint 4 had several successful features.

### 7.1 Simple Mechanism

The design was mechanically simple.

It did not require a large number of gears, bearings, brackets, or complex moving components.

This simplicity reduced:

- Part count
- Assembly complexity
- Manufacturing difficulty
- Number of possible failure points
- Space required for the mechanism

### 7.2 Belt-Driven Rotation

The belt-drive concept provided a straightforward method of transferring motor rotation to the complete Joint 5 and Joint 6 wrist module.

The motor did not need to be positioned directly on the rotational centre of Joint 4.

This allowed the motor to be mounted beside the joint while still driving the wrist rotation.

### 7.3 Basic Bearing Support

The use of bearings between the rotating wrist module and the stationary Joint 4 structure was the correct general approach.

The bearings allowed the complete Joint 5 and Joint 6 assembly to rotate while preventing direct sliding contact between the housings.

### 7.4 Adjustable Support Length

The four support rods allowed the distance between the Joint 4 support sections to be adjusted.

This made it possible to change the width of the Joint 4 structure without redesigning every surrounding component.

### 7.5 Limited Number of Components

The mechanism did not depend on many additional parts.

The main functions were completed using:

- A motor
- Two pulleys
- A belt
- Bearings
- Support rods
- A housing

This made the Joint 4 concept easier to understand and develop than some of the other joints.

## 8. Problems Identified in the First Rendition

### 8.1 Incomplete Connection Between Joint 4 and the Wrist Housing

The main problem with the first rendition was that the Joint 4 structure and the Joint 5 and Joint 6 wrist housing did not have a complete method of remaining connected.

The bearings allowed relative rotation, but there was no properly designed feature preventing the rotating wrist housing from separating axially from the Joint 4 support.

The mechanism therefore required a retention feature that would:

- Keep the two assemblies connected
- Prevent unwanted axial movement
- Allow free rotation
- Avoid clamping the rotating components together
- Maintain bearing alignment
- Allow the assembly to be removed for maintenance

A normal bolt passing through both rotating and stationary parts should not be tightened directly across the joint because this could clamp the assembly and prevent rotation.

A more suitable arrangement may use:

- A shoulder bolt
- A threaded retaining shaft
- A retaining cap
- A locknut with controlled clearance
- A retaining ring
- A bearing spacer
- A threaded end plate

The final retention method must secure the assembly through the bearing arrangement without applying unwanted friction to the rotating housing.

### 8.2 No Defined Lubrication Access

The first rendition did not include a clear method of applying lubricant to the rotating interface.

The second rendition should provide access for lubrication where required.

This could include:

- A removable threaded plug
- A lubrication access hole
- A removable bearing cover
- A service opening
- A grease port, if compatible with the selected bearings

The lubrication feature should not weaken the housing or allow lubricant to contaminate the timing belt.

Sealed bearings may reduce the need for regular lubrication, depending on the final bearing selection.

### 8.3 Excessive Support-Bracket Material

The Joint 4 support bracket used more material than necessary.

The first rendition included a large, solid-looking support structure even though most of the material did not directly contribute to the load path.

This increased:

- Joint mass
- Printing time
- Filament use
- Torque demand on Joint 3
- Overall size

The support structure should be redesigned around the actual load paths rather than remaining mostly solid.

### 8.4 Protruding Rod and Fastener Ends

The support rods and their fasteners could protrude through the opposite side of the housing.

This was undesirable because protruding hardware could:

- Interfere with surrounding components
- Reduce the available rotational clearance
- Create an unfinished appearance
- Catch cables
- Make the assembly wider than necessary

The rods should be retained without leaving unnecessary threaded sections or bolt ends exposed.

### 8.5 Inefficient Joint 3 Mounting Structure

The bottom section of the Joint 4 housing was intended to connect Joint 4 to Joint 3.

The first rendition used a large structure resembling a house-shaped frame.

This structure contained more material and geometry than was required to support the Joint 3 connection.

The Joint 3 interface only needs to:

- Follow part of the cylindrical Joint 3 geometry
- Support Joint 4 around the required contact region
- Provide a strong bolted connection
- Maintain alignment between the Joint 3 and Joint 4 axes
- Allow the required Joint 3 rotation
- Avoid interfering with the surrounding housing

A large enclosed structure was therefore unnecessary.

## 9. Improved Rotating Connection

The second rendition requires a proper connection between the stationary Joint 4 structure and the rotating Joint 5 and Joint 6 wrist housing.

The connection must perform two separate functions:

1. The bearings must allow rotation.
2. A retaining feature must prevent the assembly from separating.

The retaining feature should not become the main rotating contact surface.

A suitable arrangement should include:

- A defined shaft, shoulder, or bearing seat
- Bearings positioned around the rotational axis
- Spacers controlling the distance between bearing races
- A threaded retaining feature
- A washer, retaining cap, or locknut
- Controlled axial clearance
- Access for assembly and maintenance

The retention system should prevent axial movement while avoiding excessive preload on bearings that are not designed for it.

The second rendition should clearly show how the wrist module is installed, retained, lubricated, and removed.

---

## 10. Improved Support-Bracket Design

The Joint 4 support bracket should be redesigned to use less material while maintaining sufficient stiffness.

A suitable starting geometry would be an X-shaped internal structure surrounded by a rectangular outer frame.

The rectangular frame would provide:

- Mounting surfaces
- Bearing support
- Connection points for the rods
- Protection for the rotating assembly

The X-shaped internal members would transfer loads between the bearing area and the outer mounting points.

This arrangement could reduce mass while maintaining resistance to:

- Bending
- Twisting
- Bearing misalignment
- Separation between the support rods

The exact rib thickness and frame thickness will still need to be selected based on the expected loads and printing method.

Unnecessary solid sections should be removed from low-stress areas.

Fillets should be added where the X-shaped ribs meet the outer frame to reduce stress concentration.

---

## 11. Improved Rod Retention

The four support rods should remain because they provide a simple method of connecting and spacing the Joint 4 support sections.

However, the rod-retention method should be improved.

The second rendition should:

- Keep the rods flush with or recessed inside the surrounding structure
- Prevent the rods from rotating during assembly
- Prevent nuts or bolt ends from protruding unnecessarily
- Use washers where needed to distribute clamping force
- Avoid crushing the printed housing
- Allow the rods to be removed and replaced
- Maintain accurate spacing between the support sections

Possible retention methods include:

- Recessed nuts
- Captive nuts
- Threaded inserts
- Counterbored bolt heads
- Threaded rods with capped ends
- Rods with internal threads
- Shoulder bolts
- Cross-drilled retaining features

The final method should use standard, replaceable hardware where possible.

---

## 12. Joint 3 Mounting Interface

The lower portion of the Joint 4 support must connect to Joint 3.

The first rendition used an unnecessarily large enclosed housing.

The second rendition should use a partial cylindrical support that follows the outside geometry of the Joint 3 connection.

The improved Joint 3 mount should use an arc covering approximately 90 degrees of the cylindrical interface.

This arc would form the main support surface around the Joint 3 connection.

An external extrusion would then extend from the arc and provide a location for the connecting bolt or shaft.

The improved mounting structure should include:

- A partial cylindrical arc
- Approximately 90 degrees of support around the Joint 3 interface
- An external mounting extrusion
- A bolt or shaft connection
- Sufficient material around the bolt hole
- Clearance for Joint 3 rotation
- Proper alignment with the Joint 3 axis
- Fillets between the arc and the mounting extrusion

This structure would remove the unnecessary house-shaped housing while retaining the material required around the primary load path.

The bolt connection must be designed so that the printed material does not carry excessive concentrated load directly around an unsupported hole.

Washers, bushings, inserts, or metal spacers should be considered where the bolt passes through the printed structure.

## 13. Requirements for the Second CAD Rendition

### 13.1 Rotational Support Requirements

- The complete Joint 5 and Joint 6 wrist module must rotate around the Joint 4 axis through bearings.
- The rotating wrist housing must not rub directly against the stationary Joint 4 structure.
- One bearing race must connect to the rotating housing.
- The opposite bearing race must connect to the stationary support.
- The bearing arrangement must support the expected radial loads.
- The bearings must remain aligned during operation.
- The bearings must be retained securely inside their seats.

### 13.2 Axial Retention Requirements

- The rotating wrist assembly must not separate from the Joint 4 support.
- A dedicated retaining feature must prevent axial movement.
- The retaining feature must not prevent rotation.
- The retention system must not clamp both sides of the rotating joint together.
- Bearing spacers should be used where required.
- The retention system should be removable for maintenance.
- The assembly should use a locknut, retaining cap, shoulder bolt, retaining ring, or equivalent controlled-retention method.
- Uncontrolled loosening during operation must be prevented.

### 13.3 Lubrication and Maintenance Requirements

- The rotating interface should remain accessible for maintenance.
- Lubrication access should be included where required.
- Lubricant must not reach the timing belt.
- Bearing covers should be removable where practical.
- The selected bearings should preferably be sealed if this meets the load requirements.
- The wrist assembly should be removable without destroying the housing.

### 13.4 Motor-Mount Requirements

- The motor mount must support a NEMA 17 stepper motor.
- The four motor-mounting holes must match the selected motor.
- The motor shaft must remain parallel to the Joint 4 axis.
- The motor mount must resist belt-tension loads.
- The motor mount must not flex enough to cause belt misalignment.
- The motor screws must remain accessible.
- The motor position should allow belt installation and removal.
- The motor mount should allow some method of adjusting belt tension.

### 13.5 Pulley and Belt Requirements

- A drive pulley must be mounted securely to the NEMA 17 motor shaft.
- The drive pulley bore must match the motor shaft.
- The driven pulley must be rigidly connected to the rotating wrist housing.
- The pulleys must remain aligned.
- The belt must remain clear of the support rods and housing.
- The belt must have sufficient tooth engagement.
- The pulley ratio must provide the required Joint 4 reduction.
- The design must include a method of setting belt tension.
- The belt should be replaceable without removing every Joint 4 component.

### 13.6 Support-Rod Requirements

- Four support rods should connect the Joint 4 support sections.
- The nominal rod length should remain approximately 100 mm.
- The design should permit interchangeable rod lengths.
- The rods must not contact the rotating wrist housing.
- The rods must maintain the alignment of the bearing supports.
- Rod fasteners should remain flush or recessed where practical.
- Washers should be used where needed to distribute load.
- The rods must not crush or deform the printed housing.
- The rods should be replaceable without redesigning the complete support.

### 13.7 Lightweight Bracket Requirements

- The support bracket should use less material than the first rendition.
- The bracket should use an efficient ribbed structure.
- An X-shaped internal structure inside a rectangular frame should be considered.
- Material should remain around bearing seats, rod mounts, and fastener locations.
- Low-stress solid sections should be removed.
- The bracket must maintain bearing alignment.
- The bracket must resist bending and twisting.
- Rib intersections should use fillets.
- The bracket should be designed for practical 3D printing.

### 13.8 Joint 3 Interface Requirements

- The bottom Joint 4 support must include the Joint 3 mounting interface.
- The house-shaped structure from the first rendition should be removed.
- The interface should use a partial cylindrical arc.
- The arc should cover approximately 90 degrees of the Joint 3 connection.
- The design should include an external extrusion for the connecting bolt or shaft.
- The bolt hole must have enough surrounding material.
- The interface must allow the required Joint 3 rotation.
- The Joint 3 and Joint 4 axes must remain properly aligned.
- The connection should use washers, bushings, inserts, or spacers where required.
- The mount should avoid unnecessary mass.

## 15. Main Lessons From the First Rendition

The first rendition established a simple and practical overall concept for Joint 4.

The following design choices should be retained:

- Belt-driven rotation
- NEMA 17 motor mounting
- Bearing-supported wrist rotation
- Four adjustable support rods
- A simple circular Joint 4 housing
- A limited overall part count

The main issue was not the operating concept, but the incomplete connection and retention of the rotating wrist housing.

The first rendition allowed the wrist housing to rotate through bearings, but it did not provide a complete method of keeping the rotating and stationary structures connected without interfering with rotation.

The support bracket and Joint 3 mounting structure also used more material than necessary.

The second rendition should therefore focus on:

- Creating a proper axial-retention system
- Maintaining free bearing-supported rotation
- Adding maintenance and lubrication access
- Reducing support-bracket mass
- Improving support-rod retention
- Replacing the oversized Joint 3 housing with a partial cylindrical mount
- Maintaining the simplicity of the original mechanism

---

## 16. Final Conclusion

The first CAD rendition of Joint 4 produced a straightforward mechanism for rotating the complete Joint 5 and Joint 6 wrist assembly.

A NEMA 17 stepper motor transferred motion through a timing belt to a driven pulley connected to the wrist housing. Bearings supported the wrist assembly, while four approximately 100 mm support rods connected the surrounding Joint 4 support structures.

The general design was successful because it used few components and avoided unnecessary mechanical complexity.

However, the first rendition did not fully define how the rotating wrist housing would remain connected to the stationary Joint 4 support. A dedicated retaining feature must be added so that the assembly cannot move axially or separate while still remaining free to rotate.

The second rendition should also reduce the mass of the support bracket, prevent support-rod hardware from protruding, add maintenance access, and replace the oversized Joint 3 mounting structure with a lighter partial cylindrical arc and bolted mounting extrusion.

The main Joint 4 mechanism should remain largely unchanged. The next rendition should refine the support, retention, mounting, and assembly details so that the design is suitable for printing and physical operation.
